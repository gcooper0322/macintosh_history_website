The Macintosh (retroactively the Macintosh 128K) was introduced in January 1984.
Envisioned as an affordable, easy-to-use computer for the average consumer, it was an all-in-one unit with a built-in 9-inch monochrome CRT monitor and 400KB 3.5-inch floppy disk drive. Hardware specs included a Motorola 68000 CPU running at 8 MHz and 128KB of RAM.
Most notably, it was the first successful mass-market computer to feature a mouse-driven graphical user interface. It ran a fully graphical operating system of Apple's own design: Macintosh System Software 1.0.
The Macintosh was priced at $2,495 on launch. It initially sold well, selling 70,000 units by May 1984, but was widely criticized for its poor performance, mainly due to the small amount of RAM, and lack of an option for a second disk drive.

The 128K's successor, the Macintosh 512K, changed all that.
The 512K, introduced in September 1984, used the same overall design and had the same CPU, but quadrupled the RAM to 512KB and added an optional external floppy disk drive. The increased RAM alone made the computer much more capable of running common types of software, such as word processors and spreadsheets. It sold for $3,195.
An "enhanced" version, commonly known as the 512Ke, was introduced in April 1986 that featured an improved ROM and an 800KB floppy drive. Apple also offered an upgrade kit containing replacement ROM chips and floppy drive to upgrade a regular 512K to the 512Ke standard.

In January 1986, another improvement came along, in the form of the Macintosh Plus.
This model once again used the same design and the same CPU, but shipped with 1MB of RAM and contained module slots to expand the RAM up to 4MB. It was also the first Mac to include a SCSI port. It sold for $2,599.
