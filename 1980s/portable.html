The Macintosh Portable, as the name implies, was the first battery-powered portable Macintosh model. It was introduced in September 1989, and shipped with a 16MHz Motorola 68000 CPU, 1MB of RAM, a 40MB hard drive, and a 9.8-inch non-backlit monochrome LCD monitor in a hinged lid that covered the keyboard area when closed. It also had a modular input device system in which the built-in trackball could be placed on either the left or right side of the keyboard, or be removed entirely and replaced with a number pad if using an external mouse. The Portable's case might be described as a form of early laptop case, but the majority of the hardware sits in a bulky part of the rear case behind the display and input devices.

The Portable was criticized for being very bulky and heavy (16 pounds!) due to its use of a lead-acid battery, as well as the overall design being considered 12 to 18 months "behind the times" compared to the competition. Another issue was that the computer couldn't boot without a battery installed, due to the included power adapter not providing enough amperage by itself to spin up the hard drive. These criticisms, coupled with an initial price of $7,300, contributed to the Portable only selling 10,000 units in its first year against Apple's forecast of 50,000.

Another criticism against the Portable was that its monitor wasn't very readable in low-light environments due to the lack of a backlight. Apple introduced a backlit variant in February 1991, but the backlight, combined with a couple of other minor hardware changes, reduced the battery life by about 50%, from a claimed 10 hours to roughly 5 hours.
